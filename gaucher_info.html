Gaucher Disease

General Information

          Gaucher disease (GD) is caused by the mutation of a gene that is responsible for an enzyme called glucocerebrosidase which the body needs to break down glucocerebroside, a kind of fat. In GD, the body is not able to produce sufficient amounts of this enzyme and thus the fat is not broken down. It accumulates mostly in the liver, spleen, and bone marrow. GD is a genetic disorder prevalent among the Ashkenazi Jewish population. It affects them more when compared to the general European population. To diagnose GD, it is important to know the clinical history, conduct a physical examination, laboratory tests confirmed by a blood test showing deficient glucocerebrosidase enzyme and genetic analysis for the causal gene mutations in the GBA gene. GD can result in pain, fatigue, jaundice, bone damage, anemia, and even death. The most common form of treatment for GD is enzyme replacement which is available for most people. Such treatment helps decrease liver and spleen size, reduce skeletal abnormalities, and reverse other symptoms. On rare occasions, surgical removal of the whole or part of the spleen may be necessary. While blood transfusions are also known to benefit some anemic patients, to improve quality of life and mobility some patients may also require joint replacement surgery. There is no effective treatment for severe brain damage that may occur in certain patients with GD.
